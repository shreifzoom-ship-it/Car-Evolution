Honda: The Power of Dreams

Soichiro Honda was an extraordinary engineer with a vision. After World War II, Japan was devastated and transportation was scarce. Honda saw an opportunity to attach small motors to bicycles, creating affordable mobility. From these humble beginnings, Honda Motor Co., Ltd. was founded in 1948.

The company grew rapidly through the 1950s, becoming the world's largest motorcycle manufacturer by 1959. The famous "You meet the nicest people on a Honda" campaign transformed motorcycling's image in America from rebellious to respectable.

Honda entered automobile production in 1963 with the T360 truck and S500 sports car. But it was the 1972 Civic that changed everything. Designed during the oil crisis, the Civic was efficient, reliable, and well-built. It established Honda as a serious automaker and began the company's reputation for engineering excellence.

The 1976 Accord became a phenomenon, eventually becoming the best-selling Japanese car in America. Honda's approach was unique: instead of building small, cheap cars, they built small, high-quality cars with thoughtful engineering and excellent driving dynamics.

Honda's engineering culture produced legendary innovations: the CVCC engine that met 1970s emissions without catalytic converters, VTEC variable valve timing that gave engines dual personalities, and the NSX supercar that rewrote the rules of performance and usability.

Today, Honda is the world's largest engine manufacturer, producing everything from lawn mowers to jets. The company remains independent, family-controlled, and committed to Soichiro's vision of challenging conventions and pursuing dreams.

Iconic Models

Honda Civic (1972-present)

11 generations | 1972-present

The car that established Honda in the automotive world. The Civic has evolved from a small economy car to a global icon available as sedan, coupe, hatchback, and performance Type R. Known for reliability, efficiency, and engaging driving dynamics.

  • CVCC engine (1975) met emissions without catalytic converter
  • Type R: 306 hp, 0-60 in 5.0s
  • Best-selling car in Canada for decades
  • Over 50 mpg available in hybrid versions

Honda Accord

1976-present

The benchmark midsize sedan. The Accord defined the segment with its combination of quality, refinement, and value. Often named to "Best Cars" lists for decades.

Honda NSX

1990-2005 | 2016-present

The supercar that changed everything. Developed with Ayrton Senna, it matched Ferrari performance with Honda reliability and everyday usability.

Honda S2000

1999-2009

The ultimate roadster. Its 2.0L engine produced 240 hp - 120 hp per liter, a record at the time. 9,000 rpm redline and perfect 50:50 balance.

Honda CR-V

1995-present

The compact SUV that balanced car-like driving with utility. Consistently one of the best-selling SUVs in America.

Honda Prelude

1978-2001

The sporty coupe that introduced many to Honda performance. Featured four-wheel steering in later generations.

VTEC & Engineering Excellence

VTEC

Variable Valve Timing and Lift Electronic Control. Introduced in 1989, it gave engines two cam profiles - one for efficiency, one for high-RPM power. The VTEC "crossover" became a beloved feature.

CVCC

Compound Vortex Controlled Combustion. Honda's 1975 engine met US emissions without catalytic converters, giving Honda a huge advantage.

Aluminum Body

The NSX pioneered aluminum monocoque construction, later used in Acura and Honda models for weight reduction.

ASIMO

Honda's advanced humanoid robot showcased the company's robotics and AI research, later influencing driver assistance systems.

Hybrid Systems

Honda's IMA (Integrated Motor Assist) system was an early hybrid approach, later evolving into sophisticated two-motor systems.

Honda Sensing

Suite of advanced safety features including collision mitigation, adaptive cruise, and lane keeping, standard on most models.

The Honda Way

Honda's engineering philosophy is unique in the industry. The company encourages "waigaya" - informal, passionate discussion where hierarchy doesn't matter. Engineers are given unusual freedom to pursue ambitious projects, from the NSX to the HondaJet to ASIMO. This culture of challenging conventions has produced some of the most innovative vehicles in automotive history.

Formula 1 & Racing

Honda's racing heritage is among the most successful in history. The company's approach to racing reflects its engineering culture: innovate, challenge conventions, and pursue victory.

Formula 1 Achievements:

  • First win: 1965 Mexican GP (Richie Ginther)
  • Constructor's Championships: 1986, 1987, 1988, 1989, 1990, 1991
  • Driver's Championships: 1987 (Nelson Piquet), 1988-1991 (Ayrton Senna), 1991 (Gerhard Berger)
  • Legendary engines: RA273, RA300, RA168E (turbo), RA109E (V10)
  • Partnerships: Williams, McLaren, Lotus, Red Bull

Other Racing:

  • IndyCar: Multiple wins and championships
  • MotoGP: Over 800 victories, most successful manufacturer
  • Le Mans: Class wins with NSX
  • BTCC: Dominant with Accord in 1990s

The relationship between Honda and Ayrton Senna was particularly special. Senna's feedback directly influenced the NSX's development, and he considered it his favorite road car. Honda's racing engines have won over 200 Grands Prix, making them one of the most successful manufacturers in F1 history.

Motorcycle Legacy

Before Honda was an automaker, it was the world's greatest motorcycle company. Soichiro Honda's first product was a motorized bicycle, and motorcycles remain central to the company's identity.

Super Cub

Over 100 million sold - the most popular vehicle in history

CB750

The first superbike, revolutionized motorcycling in 1969

Goldwing

The ultimate touring motorcycle since 1975

CBR Series

Sportbike legends including the Fireblade

Honda's motorcycle division has been equally innovative, introducing the first mass-produced motorcycles with disc brakes, electric starters, and advanced aerodynamics. The company dominates MotoGP and remains the world's largest motorcycle manufacturer.
