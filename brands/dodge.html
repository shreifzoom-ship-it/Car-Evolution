Dodge: Brotherhood of Muscle

The Dodge brothers, John and Horace, started in the automotive business building engines and parts for other manufacturers – most notably Ford. Their precision engineering and quality components were so respected that Henry Ford relied on them heavily.

In 1914, the Dodge brothers launched their own car, the Dodge Model 30. It was an immediate success, praised for its all-steel body, 12-volt electrical system (most were 6-volt), and sliding-gear transmission. By 1915, Dodge was America's third best-selling brand.

Both brothers died in 1920 (John of pneumonia, Horace of cirrhosis), and the company was sold to investment bankers. In 1928, it was purchased by Walter Chrysler, becoming part of the Chrysler Corporation.

Under Chrysler, Dodge positioned itself as the sporty, performance-oriented brand. The 1950s brought the "Red Ram" HEMI V8 and stylish designs. But the 1960s and 1970s were Dodge's golden era – the muscle car years.

The 1966 Charger, 1968 Charger (with its hidden headlights and Coke-bottle styling), and 1970 Challenger defined the era. The 426 HEMI became legendary – 425 hp in an era when that was extraordinary. The Charger Daytona and Plymouth Superbird were aerodynamic monsters built for NASCAR.

The 1980s were difficult, but Dodge survived with the K-car platform and the Caravan minivan (which revolutionized family transportation). The 1992 Viper brought back performance – a brutish V10 roadster with no driver aids, pure American attitude.

In the 2000s, Dodge revived its muscle car heritage with the Charger sedan (2006) and Challenger coupe (2008). The Hellcat era (2015-present) pushed horsepower to insane levels – 707, then 797, then 840, then over 1,000 hp with Demon.

Today, Dodge is saying goodbye to its V8 muscle cars with "Last Call" editions, transitioning to electric performance with the Charger Daytona SRT EV concept. The Brotherhood of Muscle continues, but in a new form.

"Dependability – that's the foundation of Dodge quality."
- John Dodge

Iconic Models

Dodge Challenger (1970-1974, 2008-2023)

1970-1974, 2008-2023

The ultimate muscle car. The original Challenger was the last to join the pony car wars, but made up for it with style and power. The modern Challenger kept retro styling and pushed horsepower to insane levels.

  • 1970: 340, 383, 440, 426 HEMI
  • Modern: 305 hp (SE) to 807 hp (Demon 170)
  • Demon 170: 0-60 in 1.66s (production car record)
  • Quickest production car ever (quarter-mile)

The Challenger Legend

The Challenger was Dodge's entry in the pony car war, arriving in 1970 – later than Mustang, Camaro, and Barracuda. But it was worth the wait.

First Generation (1970-1974):

The original Challenger was larger and more luxurious than competitors. Engine options ranged from slant-6 to 440 Six Pack to the mighty 426 HEMI. The 1970 Challenger T/A (Trans-Am) homologation special is highly sought after.

Second Generation (2008-2023):

After a 34-year hiatus, the Challenger returned with retro styling that perfectly captured the original's spirit. It grew increasingly powerful:

  • SRT8 (2008): 425 hp 6.1L HEMI
  • SRT8 392 (2011): 470 hp 6.4L HEMI
  • SRT Hellcat (2015): 707 hp supercharged – insane
  • SRT Demon (2018): 840 hp, drag strip special
  • SRT Hellcat Redeye (2019): 797 hp
  • SRT Super Stock (2020): 807 hp
  • Demon 170 (2023): 1,025 hp on E85 – quickest production car ever

The Challenger ended production in 2023 with "Last Call" special editions – the end of an era for V8 muscle.

Charger – From Coupe to Sedan

First Generation (1966-1967):

The original Charger was a fastback coupe with hidden headlights and a full-width taillight. Fast and stylish.

Second Generation (1968-1970):

The iconic Charger. Coke-bottle styling, hidden headlights (1970 had exposed), and the "General Lee" fame. The 1968-1970 Charger is one of the most beautiful muscle cars ever.

Third Generation (1971-1974):

Larger, more rounded, still powerful but emissions were coming.

Fourth/Fifth Generations (1975-1978):

Personal luxury coupes, not muscle cars.

Sixth Generation (2006-present):

The Charger returned as a four-door sedan – controversial but brilliant. It offered V8 power and rear drive in a practical package. Hellcat versions made it the world's most powerful sedan.

The Charger has been featured in countless films and TV shows, most famously "The Dukes of Hazzard" (1969 Charger as General Lee) and "Bullitt" (1968 Charger vs Mustang).

The Viper – American Supercar

The Viper was born from a simple idea: an American answer to the AC Cobra. No driver aids, massive power, and raw, visceral experience.

Generations:

  • SR I (1992-1995): RT/10 roadster. 8.0L V10, 400 hp, no roof, no windows, no driver aids. Insane.
  • SR II (1996-2002): GTS coupe with blue stripes. 450 hp, more refined but still raw.
  • ZB I (2003-2006): SRT-10. 8.3L, 500 hp, convertible and coupe. More civilized.
  • ZB II (2008-2010): 8.4L, 600 hp. ACR version set lap records.
  • VX I (2013-2017): Final generation. 8.4L, 645 hp, ACR with insane aero. The ultimate Viper.

The Viper ACR (American Club Racer) was a track monster, holding lap records at many tracks. It had more downforce than any production car – you could drive it upside down theoretically.

Production ended in 2017, but the Viper's legacy as America's most extreme supercar lives on.

The HEMI Engine

The HEMI engine is legendary. Its hemispherical combustion chambers allow for better airflow, more power, and that distinctive sound.

Generations:

  • First Generation (1951-1958): FirePower V8s for Chrysler, DeSoto, Dodge. 331, 354, 392 cubic inches.
  • Second Generation (1964-1971): The legendary 426 HEMI. 425 hp, dominated NASCAR and drag racing. Street HEMI option in 1966-1971.
  • Third Generation (2003-present): Modern HEMI. 5.7L, 6.1L, 6.2L supercharged Hellcat. Variable valve timing, MDS cylinder deactivation.

Hellcat Family:

  • Hellcat (2015): 6.2L supercharged, 707 hp
  • Hellcat Redeye (2019): 797 hp
  • Super Stock (2020): 807 hp
  • Demon (2018): 840 hp, race gas
  • Demon 170 (2023): 1,025 hp on E85

The HEMI name is synonymous with American power. Its distinctive sound – that lumpy idle – is music to enthusiasts.

Dodge Racing Heritage

  • NASCAR: Charger Daytona and Plymouth Superbird dominated in 1970 with aerodynamic wings. Modern Charger has wins.
  • Drag Racing: HEMI-powered Dodges have dominated NHRA for decades. Factory drag cars like Dart GTS, Demon.
  • Trans-Am: Challenger T/A raced in 1970.
  • IMSA / Sports Car: Viper GTS-R won class at Le Mans (1998, 1999, 2000) and multiple championships.
  • SRT Engineering: Factory performance division created Viper, SRT models.

The Viper's racing success was remarkable. It dominated GT racing for years, beating European exotics on their own turf.
